# PRD 19: ExitBridge — Multinational Exit Talent & Gap Marketplace

## 1. Executive Summary

ExitBridge is a two-sided marketplace connecting experienced professionals displaced by multinational exits from Nigeria with local businesses seeking to hire that talent, fill distribution gaps, or acquire supplier relationships. P&G, GSK (after 51 years), Unilever, Sanofi, Kimberly-Clark, and Diageo have all exited or are exiting Nigeria, leaving behind thousands of highly trained professionals — supply chain managers who ran continental logistics operations, sales directors who built distribution networks across 36 states, quality assurance engineers trained to ISO standards, and finance controllers who managed multi-billion-naira P&Ls. These professionals are now available at local salary rates, but the window to absorb them is closing fast: 3.68 million Nigerians emigrated in just two years, and 69% of 18-35 year olds are willing to relocate abroad. Every week that passes, more multinational-trained talent "japas" — and that knowledge, those relationships, and those capabilities leave Nigeria permanently. ExitBridge creates urgency and structure around this absorption. On one side, professionals from exiting multinationals list their skills, industry contacts, distribution knowledge, and availability. On the other side, Nigerian businesses — manufacturers looking to replace imported products with local alternatives, distributors seeking experienced sales teams, and companies needing to fill the product gaps left by departing brands — browse, filter, and connect. Hiring businesses pay N50-100K per connection or N200K/month for unlimited access. The MVP is a straightforward marketplace that can ship in 3-4 weeks, but the business model carries a fundamental tension: the exit wave is time-limited, the talent pool is finite and shrinking, and once absorbed or emigrated, the supply side disappears. ExitBridge is a window-dependent opportunity that must move fast to capture value before the window closes.

## 2. Market Opportunity

**A once-in-a-generation talent arbitrage is happening right now, and the clock is ticking.**

- **Multinational exits accelerating:** P&G, GSK (51 years in Nigeria), Unilever, Sanofi, Kimberly-Clark, Diageo, and others have exited or announced exits. These are not struggling startups — they are Fortune 500 companies that built deep operational capability in Nigeria over decades and are now walking away from it.
- **Job losses concentrated in manufacturing:** 18,000 manufacturing jobs lost in 2024 alone. These are not entry-level positions — multinational manufacturers employ experienced engineers, supply chain specialists, quality managers, and commercial leaders who are difficult and expensive to train.
- **767 companies shut down by 2023:** The wave is broad. Beyond the headline multinationals, hundreds of mid-tier foreign-owned businesses have also closed, releasing professionals across every function.
- **Talent quality at local pricing:** A supply chain director who spent 15 years at Unilever earning N25-40M/year is now available to a Nigerian manufacturer at N8-15M/year. The salary gap creates extraordinary value for Nigerian businesses that move quickly to hire.
- **Distribution gaps:** When GSK exits, someone must fill the pharmaceutical distribution gap. When Kimberly-Clark exits, the diaper and tissue distribution networks become available. Nigerian businesses that fill these gaps capture market share from departing brands — but they need the people who understand those distribution networks.
- **The "japa" drain accelerating:** 3.68 million Nigerians emigrated in two years. 69% of 18-35 year olds are willing to relocate. Brain drain is not hypothetical — it is the default outcome. Without a structured absorption mechanism, multinational-trained talent leaves Nigeria within 6-12 months of displacement.
- **No structured mechanism exists:** LinkedIn is generic and not optimized for this specific transition. Recruitment agencies charge 2-3 months' salary (N5-30M per senior hire) and are slow. There is no purpose-built platform connecting exit-wave talent with Nigerian businesses seeking exactly this talent profile.
- **Expansion appetite without hiring infrastructure:** Businesses want to expand (index 54-81) but struggle to hire (index 8-40). The gap is partly about cost and regulation (62 tax levies make each hire 18%+ more expensive) but also about finding qualified people. 85% of graduates lack digital competencies. Multinational-trained professionals are the exception — they have the skills businesses need.

**The wedge:** ExitBridge is not a general recruitment platform. It is a time-limited, event-driven marketplace built around a specific market dislocation. The value proposition is clear: "The best-trained professionals in Nigeria just became available. Here's where to find them before they leave the country."

## 3. Target Persona

**Primary: The MD/CEO of a Mid-Sized Nigerian Manufacturer or Distributor**

- **Company profile:** Nigerian-owned manufacturer or distributor in Food/Beverage, personal care, pharmaceuticals, building materials, or consumer goods. 50-500 employees. N100M-1B/month revenue ($65K-$650K/month). Currently growing and facing talent constraints.
- **Strategic context:** Sees the multinational exits as an opportunity. Wants to capture market share in product categories being vacated (e.g., local tissue brand replacing Kimberly-Clark, local pharma distributor replacing GSK's distribution network). Needs experienced people to execute this expansion.
- **Current hiring process:** Uses personal networks, recruitment agencies (expensive, slow), and LinkedIn (generic, not Nigeria-specific). Has tried to poach from multinationals before but lacked a systematic approach. Now that the talent is being released en masse, needs a way to find and evaluate candidates quickly.
- **Title:** Managing Director, CEO, COO, or VP of Operations. In companies actively pursuing an acquisition or distribution strategy, the decision-maker is the MD personally.
- **Age:** 35-55. Experienced business leader with industry relationships. Uses WhatsApp for 80%+ of business communication. Active on LinkedIn for professional networking.
- **Budget authority:** Can approve N200K-500K/month for hiring tools without board approval. Willing to pay premium for access to verified multinational talent — the cost of a bad hire at this level is N10-30M.
- **Decision trigger:** Learns that a competitor just hired GSK's former regional sales manager and is now winning distribution contracts. Or discovers that a key talent they wanted to approach has already emigrated to the UK. Fear of missing out on a closing window.

**Secondary: HR Directors at Larger Nigerian Companies**

Companies with 500+ employees that have formal HR departments and hiring budgets. They want a pipeline of pre-vetted, multinational-trained candidates without paying recruitment agency fees of N5-30M per placement.

**Tertiary: Entrepreneurs Seeking Distribution Opportunities**

Business people who want to acquire or build distribution for product categories being vacated by departing multinationals. They need both the talent (people who understand the distribution networks) and the relationships (contacts at retailers, suppliers, logistics providers).

**Priority sectors:** Food/Beverage manufacturers (63.24% capacity, highest stability, actively expanding). Construction sector (80.8 expansion index — highest growth outlook). Avoid Electrical/Electronics (28% capacity, collapsing).

## 4. Problem Deep Dive

**The current state of multinational talent absorption in Nigeria:**

**Step 1: The Exit Announcement**
A multinational announces it is exiting Nigeria. Depending on the company, this involves selling the business to a local acquirer (who may retain some staff), winding down operations entirely (all staff displaced), or transitioning to a distributor model (commercial staff retained, manufacturing staff displaced). The announcement creates immediate uncertainty for hundreds or thousands of employees.

**Step 2: The Talent Scatters**
Displaced professionals respond in one of four ways:
- **Japa (emigration):** The most skilled and mobile professionals — often the ones Nigerian businesses most need — begin applying for international positions within days. Senior managers with international experience are the first to leave. They have options, contacts abroad, and transferable skills.
- **LinkedIn passive mode:** Others update their LinkedIn profiles, change their headline to "Open to opportunities," and wait. In a market with limited recruiter activity, they wait a long time. Their skills depreciate. Their industry contacts go stale.
- **Network-based job search:** The most connected professionals find new roles through personal networks. This works for individuals but does not systematically match supply with demand across the economy.
- **Entrepreneurship (often by necessity):** Some start businesses, often in areas unrelated to their multinational experience, wasting years of specialized training.

**Step 3: The Information Failure**
Meanwhile, Nigerian businesses that would benefit enormously from this talent cannot find it:
- **They do not know who is available.** A manufacturer in Aba who needs a quality assurance manager does not know that a former P&G QA director in Lagos just became available.
- **They cannot assess fit.** Even when they learn about a candidate, evaluating whether a multinational professional will adapt to a Nigerian SME environment is difficult. Multinational experience can mean "world-class operator" or "cannot function without a 50-person support team."
- **They cannot move fast enough.** By the time a company identifies, evaluates, negotiates with, and hires a displaced professional, the best candidates have already accepted offers elsewhere or emigrated.
- **Recruitment agencies are misaligned.** Traditional agencies charge 2-3 months' salary, which prices out mid-sized Nigerian companies. Agencies also lack domain expertise in matching multinational-trained talent to local business contexts.

**Step 4: The Distribution Gap Problem**
Beyond individual talent, the multinational exits create distribution gaps that are even harder to fill:
- GSK distributed pharmaceuticals through a network built over 51 years. When GSK leaves, who inherits those retail pharmacy relationships?
- Kimberly-Clark's tissue and diaper products were distributed through specific channels. Nigerian manufacturers can produce alternatives but need the distribution knowledge.
- P&G's FMCG distribution covered every major market in Nigeria. That institutional knowledge — which route salespeople served which areas, which distributors were reliable, what terms each retailer expected — lives in the heads of displaced employees.

This distribution knowledge is perishable. Once the former employees scatter, emigrate, or forget the details, it is gone permanently.

**Step 5: The Window Closes**
The talent arbitrage has a natural expiration:
- **3-6 months post-exit:** Best talent still available but actively exploring options. This is the highest-value window.
- **6-12 months post-exit:** Remaining talent has either found positions, emigrated, or begun losing relevance as industry contacts go stale and skills are not exercised.
- **12+ months post-exit:** The pool is largely depleted. Those who remain have either been absorbed or have moved to different career paths. The distribution knowledge they carried has deteriorated.

ExitBridge must capture value in the 3-12 month window following each major exit. This is not a steady-state business — it is a time-sensitive extraction of value from a market dislocation.

## 5. Solution Overview

ExitBridge is a web-based marketplace platform that creates structured connections between multinational-displaced talent and Nigerian businesses seeking that talent. The system operates in three layers:

**1. Talent Profiles (Supply Side)**
Professionals from exiting multinationals create detailed profiles that go beyond a standard resume:
- **Career history** with emphasis on Nigerian market experience, distribution networks managed, supplier relationships built, and operational capabilities developed.
- **Skills matrix** tagged to specific functions (supply chain, sales/distribution, manufacturing/QA, finance, HR, regulatory) and industries (FMCG, pharma, consumer goods, industrial).
- **Network value declaration:** Which retailers, distributors, suppliers, and logistics providers does this person have active relationships with? This is the most valuable asset — a former GSK sales director's Rolodex of pharmacy contacts is worth more than their resume.
- **Availability and terms:** Available immediately or on notice? Open to relocation within Nigeria? Salary expectations? Willing to consult part-time while exploring full-time roles?
- **Verification:** LinkedIn profile cross-reference, former employer confirmation (where possible), and professional reference contacts.

**2. Business Search & Discovery (Demand Side)**
Hiring businesses search and discover talent through multiple paths:
- **Function-based search:** "I need a supply chain director with FMCG experience in South-West Nigeria."
- **Company-based browse:** "Show me all available professionals from GSK's Nigeria operation."
- **Distribution gap search:** "Who has experience distributing pharmaceutical products in Northern Nigeria?"
- **Skill-based filter:** ISO certification experience, SAP/Oracle proficiency, regulatory compliance in specific sectors.
- **Urgency indicator:** Profiles flagged as "actively interviewing internationally" or "considering emigration" to create urgency for local buyers.

**3. Connection & Placement**
When a business finds a relevant professional:
- **Express interest:** Business requests an introduction. The professional is notified (WhatsApp + email + in-app).
- **Mutual match:** If the professional accepts, contact details are exchanged (this is the monetization event — paying per connection).
- **Interview facilitation:** The platform provides a scheduling tool and interview guide tailored to evaluating multinational talent for local business contexts.
- **Placement tracking:** Track whether connections result in hires. Collect outcome data to improve matching and demonstrate platform value.

## 7. Key Screens & UX

### Design Principles

- **Urgency everywhere:** The window is closing. Every screen should reinforce that this talent is available NOW but won't be for long. Countdown timers, "last active" dates, and emigration risk indicators create urgency.
- **Network value visible:** The most valuable thing about multinational-trained professionals is not their resume — it is their relationships. Profile design must surface network value (distribution contacts, supplier relationships, retailer connections) prominently.
- **WhatsApp-native communication:** Connection requests, responses, and follow-ups all flow through WhatsApp. The platform orchestrates; WhatsApp delivers.
- **Numbers forward:** For businesses: "47 GSK professionals available, 12 actively interviewing abroad." For talent: "3 businesses interested in your profile this week."

### Screen 1: Business Dashboard (Demand Side)

The landing screen for hiring businesses after login.

**Header KPIs:**
- **Available Talent:** Total number of active profiles matching the business's declared needs. Example: "234 professionals in FMCG supply chain"
- **New This Week:** Profiles added in the last 7 days. Example: "18 new profiles"
- **Urgent (Flight Risk):** Profiles flagged as actively exploring international opportunities. Example: "12 actively interviewing abroad"
- **Your Connections:** Total connections made, pending responses, successful hires. Example: "5 connected / 3 responded / 1 hired"

**Body:**
- **Recommended Profiles:** AI-matched (post-MVP) or manually curated based on the business's declared needs. Carousel of 5-8 profile cards.
- **Recent Exits Tracker:** Timeline showing recent multinational exit announcements with estimated talent release dates and profile counts.
- **Search bar:** Prominent search input with suggested filters.

### Screen 2: Talent Search & Results

Full-width search and filter interface.

**Filter sidebar:**
- Function: Supply Chain, Sales/Distribution, Manufacturing/QA, Finance, HR, Regulatory, IT, Marketing
- Former employer: Dropdown of exiting multinationals (P&G, GSK, Unilever, Sanofi, Kimberly-Clark, Diageo, etc.)
- Seniority: Director/VP, Manager, Senior Individual Contributor, Individual Contributor
- Location: Lagos, Port Harcourt, Abuja, Kano, Other (with relocation willingness filter)
- Industry experience: FMCG, Pharma, Consumer Goods, Industrial, Building Materials
- Availability: Immediately available, 1 month notice, 3 month notice
- Urgency: All / Flight risk only

**Results grid:**
Profile cards showing: Name (first name + initial only until connection), former company + title, function, years of experience, location, availability status, urgency badge (if applicable), network value summary (e.g., "Managed 200+ retail pharmacy relationships in South-West").

**Actions per card:**
- "View Full Profile" → opens detailed profile
- "Connect" → initiates connection request (payment required)
- "Save" → adds to shortlist

### Screen 3: Talent Profile (Detailed View)

**Header:**
- Name (first name + initial until connection, full name after)
- Photo (if uploaded)
- Former company and title
- Function and seniority badges
- Availability status and urgency indicator
- "Connect" button (prominent, top-right)

**Section A — Professional Summary:**
3-5 sentence overview written by the professional. Guided by prompts: "What did you build at [Company]? What results did you deliver? What do you want to do next?"

**Section B — Career History:**
Timeline of roles with company, title, dates, and 2-3 bullet points per role. Emphasis on Nigerian market achievements.

**Section C — Skills Matrix:**
Tagged skills with self-assessed proficiency (Expert, Advanced, Intermediate). Organized by category: Technical, Operational, Commercial, Regulatory.

**Section D — Network Value (the key differentiator):**
- Distribution channels managed: "200+ retail pharmacies in Lagos, Ogun, Oyo"
- Supplier relationships: "Direct relationships with 15 API suppliers in India and China"
- Retailer/customer contacts: "Key account relationships at Shoprite, SPAR, and 50+ independent supermarkets"
- Industry association memberships: NAFDAC contacts, SON relationships, MAN membership
- Anonymized until connection is established (e.g., "Managed relationships with [X] retail outlets across [Y] states")

**Section E — Preferences:**
- Desired role type: Full-time, consulting, advisory, distribution partnership
- Salary range: Displayed as a band (e.g., "N10-15M/year")
- Location flexibility: Will relocate / Lagos only / Remote
- Industry openness: Same industry only / Open to related industries

**Section F — Verification:**
Badges: LinkedIn Verified, Employer Confirmed, References Available, Profile Complete

### Screen 4: Talent Dashboard (Supply Side)

The landing screen for professionals after login.

**Header KPIs:**
- **Profile Views:** How many businesses viewed their profile this week. Example: "14 views"
- **Connection Requests:** Pending requests from businesses. Example: "3 new requests"
- **Market Pulse:** How many businesses are actively hiring in their function. Example: "28 companies hiring supply chain professionals"

**Body:**
- **Pending Requests:** Cards showing requesting business name, industry, role description, and urgency. Accept/Decline buttons.
- **Profile Completeness:** Progress bar with prompts to fill missing sections. "Complete your Network Value section — businesses are 3x more likely to connect with profiles that show distribution relationships."
- **Tips:** "Profiles with verified LinkedIn see 2x more connection requests."

### Screen 5: Connection Management

For businesses managing their connections pipeline.

**Table columns:** Professional Name, Former Company, Function, Connection Date, Status (Pending/Accepted/Declined/Hired), Last Activity, Notes.

**Status flow:**
- Pending → professional has not responded
- Accepted → contact details exchanged, interview stage
- Declined → professional not interested
- Hired → outcome tracked (self-reported by business)

**Actions:** Send WhatsApp follow-up, add notes, mark as hired, export to Excel.

### Search Architecture

```
Talent Search Flow:

1. User enters search query + applies filters

2. Structured Filter Query (fast):
   - WHERE function = 'supply_chain'
   - AND former_company IN ('GSK', 'P&G')
   - AND seniority IN ('director_vp', 'manager')
   - AND location = 'Lagos'
   - AND is_active = true

3. Full-Text Search (for keyword queries):
   - Use PostgreSQL tsvector on: professional_summary, skills,
     network_value, career_history
   - Weight: network_value (A), skills (B), professional_summary (C),
     career_history (D)

4. Ranking:
   - Full-text relevance score
   - Profile completeness (higher = better)
   - Verification status (verified = boost)
   - Last active date (recent = boost)
   - Emigration risk (high risk = boost for urgency)

5. Results:
   - Return paginated results (20 per page)
   - Show match score as relevance percentage
   - Highlight matching keywords in profile snippets
```

### Notification Architecture

```
Connection Request Flow:
  Business clicks "Connect" → Paystack payment → Payment confirmed →
  Connection record created → Notification dispatched:
    1. WhatsApp message to talent (via Termii): "A [Industry] company
       in [Location] wants to connect with you about a [Function] role.
       Log in to ExitBridge to respond."
    2. Email to talent (via Resend): Detailed connection request with
       business profile summary
    3. In-app notification: Badge on dashboard

  Talent responds (Accept/Decline) → Notification to business:
    1. WhatsApp: "[Talent Name] accepted your connection request.
       Contact details: [Phone/Email]"
    2. Email: Full profile with contact details
    3. In-app: Connection status updated

  If no response in 48 hours:
    1. Reminder WhatsApp to talent
    2. After 7 days: Auto-expire, refund business
```

```

  - Own profile data (for talent)

Not cached (requires network):
  - New searches (require database query)
  - Connection requests (require payment)
  - Real-time profile updates
  - WhatsApp/email notifications
```

## 11. Risks & Mitigations

### Risk 1: The Window Closes Faster Than Expected

**Likelihood:** Medium-High. If the best talent emigrates within 3-6 months of displacement and no new multinational exits occur, the supply side evaporates.

**Impact:** Marketplace loses its supply. Revenue peaks early and declines. The business has a natural expiration date.

**Mitigations:**
- Speed is the primary mitigation. Launch fast, build the talent pool immediately, and create connections before talent emigrates.
- Track emigration intent in profiles. Proactively notify businesses when high-value talent is at flight risk ("This former GSK director is interviewing with a UK company. Connect now or lose access.").
- Expand definition beyond multinational exits. Local companies also lay off experienced professionals. Nigerian banks restructuring, oil companies downsizing, tech companies cutting staff — all produce available talent.
- Build the distribution opportunity side as a hedge. Even if individual talent emigrates, distribution gaps persist. The marketplace evolves from "find people" to "find opportunities."
- If the window does close, pivot: the talent database, business relationships, and marketplace infrastructure can serve the broader Nigerian professional hiring market.

### Risk 2: Cold Start — Chicken-and-Egg Marketplace Problem

**Likelihood:** High. Businesses will not pay until they see quality talent. Talent will not create profiles until they see business demand.

**Impact:** Marketplace never achieves liquidity. Both sides wait for the other.

**Mitigations:**
- Build supply first (Phase 0 in Go-to-Market). Free profiles, LinkedIn outreach, alumni group seeding. Target 100+ profiles before charging businesses.
- Manually curate the first 20-30 profiles for quality. Ghost-write profiles for displaced professionals if needed (with their permission and data).
- Offer 1 free connection to every business signup. Reduce the risk of the first transaction.
- Share anonymized talent summaries in outreach: "We have 15 former GSK sales directors with 200+ retail pharmacy contacts in Lagos. Want access?" Prove supply exists before asking for payment.
- Concentration strategy: focus on ONE multinational exit first (e.g., GSK). Build deep supply in one pool rather than thin supply across all exits.

### Risk 3: LinkedIn Already Serves This Function

**Likelihood:** Medium. LinkedIn is where professionals list their experience and businesses search for talent. Why wouldn't businesses just use LinkedIn?

**Impact:** Businesses see ExitBridge as redundant. Low willingness to pay for a marketplace when LinkedIn is "free."

**Mitigations:**
- ExitBridge offers what LinkedIn does not: network value declarations (distribution contacts, supplier relationships), exit-specific urgency indicators, and a curated pool of multinational-trained talent with Nigerian context.
- LinkedIn is a haystack. ExitBridge is the needle. Searching "supply chain manager FMCG Nigeria" on LinkedIn returns thousands of results with no way to filter by former employer exit status, distribution network value, or emigration risk.
- Per-connection pricing is cheaper than LinkedIn Recruiter (N50K vs. N150K+/month for Recruiter Lite) and more targeted.
- The real competition is not LinkedIn — it is recruitment agencies charging N5-30M per placement. ExitBridge at N50-100K per connection is 98% cheaper.

### Risk 4: Low Willingness to Pay Per Connection

**Likelihood:** Medium. Nigerian businesses may balk at paying N50-100K just to exchange contact details with a professional they have not yet evaluated.

**Impact:** Low conversion from browse to connect. Revenue per user lower than projected.

**Mitigations:**
- 1 free connection on signup reduces the perceived risk of the first transaction.
- "Declined = 50% refund" policy reduces the risk of paying for a connection that goes nowhere.
- Show enough profile detail (skills, experience, network value summary) to build confidence before the paywall. Only hide direct contact details.
- Unlimited subscription (N200K/month) is a better deal for businesses hiring multiple positions — position it as the default for serious buyers.
- Track and publish success metrics: "72% of connections result in an interview. 34% result in a hire." Prove the connection fee delivers ROI.

### Risk 5: Talent Quality Varies Widely

**Likelihood:** Medium. Not everyone displaced by a multinational is a star performer. Some were let go for performance reasons, not just because the company exited.

**Impact:** Businesses connect with low-quality talent, experience disappointment, and churn.

**Mitigations:**
- Verification badges: LinkedIn cross-reference (did they actually work at the company they claim?), employer confirmation (where available), reference contacts.
- Profile completeness as a quality signal. Detailed profiles with specific achievements and network value are more credible than sparse ones.
- Post-connection feedback: businesses rate the quality of each connection. Low-rated profiles get flagged for review.
- Curated "Editor's Picks" for the first 50 profiles — manually verified, high-quality profiles that set the standard for the marketplace.
- Seniority-based pricing naturally filters: businesses paying N100K for a director-level connection have higher expectations, which pushes the marketplace toward quality.

### Risk 6: Ethical Concerns About Monetizing Displacement

**Likelihood:** Low-Medium. Some may view ExitBridge as profiting from people's job losses.

**Impact:** Negative PR, talent reluctance to participate, business hesitation.

**Mitigations:**
- Position ExitBridge as a solution, not an exploitation. "Preventing brain drain" and "keeping Nigerian talent in Nigeria" is the narrative.
- Free for talent. Professionals pay nothing. The marketplace charges businesses that benefit from hiring, not professionals who need jobs.
- Publish impact metrics: "ExitBridge has facilitated 200 local hires, preventing N500M in talent from leaving Nigeria."
- Partner with NGOs and government agencies focused on employment. Frame ExitBridge as national economic infrastructure.

### Verdict: CONDITIONAL BUILD (Low Priority)

A 5.60 composite score places ExitBridge below the build threshold for a solo founder with limited bandwidth. The fundamental tension is between the time-sensitive opportunity (the window IS real and IS closing) and the structural weaknesses (low skill fit at 4, low fintech upside at 3, finite market at 5). This is a good business idea for someone — but not necessarily for a solo AI/data science engineer optimizing for long-term fintech platform value.

**Key decision factors:**
- The window-dependent nature is both the strength and the weakness. The urgency creates buyer motivation, but it also means the business has a natural expiration date.
- Skill fit is the lowest score in the portfolio. Building a marketplace does not leverage core strengths in ML/data science. Every week spent on ExitBridge is a week not spent on VendorScore (skill fit: 8) or PayMatch (skill fit: 9).
- Fintech upside at 3 means ExitBridge is a standalone business, not a stepping stone to a financial services platform. It does not compound into other products in the portfolio.
- The cold-start marketplace problem is real and requires significant outreach effort on both sides simultaneously. For a solo founder, this splits attention at the worst possible time.

**Build condition:** Only build if (a) PayMatch and VendorScore are already generating revenue, (b) you can recruit a non-technical co-founder or contractor to handle the supply-side outreach and talent curation, and (c) the multinational exit wave shows no signs of slowing. If all three conditions are met by Month 6, ExitBridge can be a valuable side project. Otherwise, skip.

**Alternative:** Build a lightweight version as a feature inside an existing product or community. A "Talent Board" page on a Nigerian business newsletter or WhatsApp community costs near-zero and tests demand without a full marketplace build. If the feature gains traction, invest in the full platform.
