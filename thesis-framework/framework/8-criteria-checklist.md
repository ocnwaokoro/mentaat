# 8 Criteria for Disruptive Innovation

A disruptive innovation is any innovation grounded in a well-specified problem that meets ALL of the following. Use this as a sequential filter — if an idea fails early criteria, later ones are unlikely to save it.

---

## 1. Targets Overlooked or Non-consuming Segments

**Question:** Who is currently unable to access or afford the existing solution? Who has been priced out, ignored, or never served?

- The target is NOT mainstream customers who already buy from incumbents
- You can clearly name the non-consumers or underserved group
- These people have a real need but are currently making do with nothing, or with a vastly inferior workaround

**Red flag:** If your primary customer is someone already well-served by existing products, this is sustaining innovation, not disruptive.

> *Indomie example:* 200M+ Nigerians who had never seen noodles. Not competing for restaurant customers — creating eaters who could now afford a filling meal for 18 cents.

---

## 2. Appears Deliberately "Worse" (but Good Enough) at First

**Question:** Would an incumbent's best customers look at your product and say "that's inferior"? And would your target customers say "this works for me"?

- The product sacrifices features, polish, or performance that mainstream customers value
- It compensates with accessibility, simplicity, affordability, or convenience
- "Good enough" means it solves the core problem — nothing more

**Red flag:** If you're trying to be the best product on the market from day one, you're doing sustaining or breakthrough innovation.

> *Indomie example:* Nigerians initially thought they were being sold worms. The product was foreign, unfamiliar, and simpler than a home-cooked meal. But it was filling, cheap, and fast.

---

## 3. Gains Traction via Low-End or New-Market Foothold

**Question:** Which foothold applies?

**Low-end foothold:**
- Enters at the bottom of an existing market
- Cheaper, simpler version of something that already exists
- Over-served mainstream customers don't need all the features they're paying for

**New-market foothold:**
- Creates a category that didn't exist before
- Turns non-consumers into first-time buyers
- No direct incumbent to displace initially

**Both can apply simultaneously** (Indomie had both — cheaper than existing fast food AND a completely new food category).

**Red flag:** If you're entering at the premium end of an existing market, this isn't disruption.

---

## 4. Reimagines the Value Chain

**Question:** Does delivering this product require you to build, source, distribute, or operate differently than incumbents?

- The product alone is not the innovation — the system around it is
- New logistics, new supplier relationships, new distribution channels
- May require building infrastructure that doesn't exist yet

**Red flag:** If you can deliver this through existing supply chains and distribution with no changes, it's probably a sustaining innovation on an existing product.

> *Indomie example:* Tolaram built factories, power systems, water infrastructure, a 1,000-vehicle logistics fleet, trained workers in-house, created 600K retail points. The noodle was simple — the value chain was the real innovation.

See: [value-chain-design.md](value-chain-design.md)

---

## 5. Prioritizes Scaling a Core Solution

**Question:** Are you focused on maximum reach for a baseline product, or perfecting features for a niche?

- The goal is distribution breadth, not product depth
- Ship the simplest version that solves the core problem
- Features come later, after you've achieved reach

**Red flag:** If your roadmap is feature-heavy before you've achieved meaningful distribution, you're optimizing for the wrong thing.

> *Indomie example:* Started with one generic Indonesian flavor. Only added Nigeria-specific spicy variants after achieving national distribution.

---

## 6. Demands a Different Cost Structure

**Question:** Can you operate profitably at price points incumbents would find unattractive?

- Your cost model is fundamentally different — not just "cheaper"
- Volume and affordability over high margins
- May require different labor models, raw material sources, or operational approaches

**Red flag:** If your unit economics only work at premium pricing, you can't serve the overlooked segment.

> *Indomie example:* 18 cents per pack. Annual food cost of under $200. This was only viable because of local manufacturing, in-house logistics, and massive scale (4.5B packs/year).

See: [cost-structure-patterns.md](cost-structure-patterns.md)

---

## 7. Converts Non-consumption into Growth

**Question:** Does your growth come from people who previously had NOTHING in this category, or from stealing share from competitors?

- Total addressable market expands because of your product
- New jobs, new supply chains, new economic activity follow
- Social/economic impact is a natural byproduct, not a marketing angle

**Red flag:** If your growth model is purely market share theft from incumbents, it's competitive innovation, not disruptive.

> *Indomie example:* 8,500 direct jobs. 1,000 exclusive distributors. 600K retailers. Tens of millions in tax revenue. A logistics company serving external clients. An industrial free trade zone. All from selling noodles to people who previously had no access to convenient, affordable meals.

---

## 8. Can Mature to Surpass Mainstream Offerings

**Question:** Is there a credible path from "good enough" to "better than what existed before"?

- The product improves iteratively over time
- Eventually meets or exceeds mainstream quality standards
- Can move upmarket to serve previously skeptical customer segments

**Red flag:** If the product is permanently limited and can never improve beyond its initial state, it may not sustain disruption.

> *Indomie example:* From one imported flavor to locally manufactured, quality-controlled products with Nigeria-specific variants. Import ban enabled quality standards that surpassed some international counterparts (e.g., avoiding the 2023 toxic ingredient scandal in Taiwanese/Malaysian noodles).

---

## Scoring Your Idea

For each criterion, rate:
- **Strong fit** — clear evidence this applies
- **Partial fit** — some elements present, gaps identified
- **No fit** — this criterion is not met
- **Unknown** — need more research

A truly disruptive innovation should score "strong fit" on at least 6 of 8, with no "no fit" on criteria 1, 3, or 7 (these are non-negotiable).
