# Cost Structure Patterns

Disruptive innovations demand fundamentally different economics — not just "cheaper," but structured differently.

---

## The Core Constraint

Your target customer is in the overlooked/non-consuming segment. They have limited purchasing power. Your cost structure must make profitability possible at price points that incumbents would consider unattractive.

**The math:** If incumbents need $10 margins to cover their overhead, and your customer can only pay $0.18, you don't just need to be cheaper — you need a completely different cost model.

---

## Pattern 1: Volume Over Margin

- Razor-thin per-unit profit, massive volume
- Requires distribution infrastructure to support scale
- Tolaram: 18 cents/pack × 4.5 billion packs/year = ~$1B revenue

**When it works:** When the addressable market is enormous (200M+ people in Nigeria) and the product is consumable/recurring.

**What it requires:** Logistics and distribution that can handle massive throughput at low cost per unit.

---

## Pattern 2: Waste-as-Input

- Raw material is someone else's disposal problem
- Your input cost is near zero or even negative (they pay you to take it)
- Henry Ford: sawmill waste → charcoal briquettes
- PWCB: palm kernel shells (waste product) → cooking fuel

**When it works:** When an adjacent industry produces abundant waste that can be converted with relatively simple processing.

**What it requires:** Reliable waste supply, processing infrastructure, and solving the logistics of collection.

---

## Pattern 3: Build Infrastructure, Then Monetize It

- Invest in infrastructure (logistics, power, training) to support your core product
- Once built, that infrastructure serves other businesses for additional revenue
- Tolaram's logistics arm: 65% of revenue from external clients

**When it works:** When the infrastructure you need doesn't exist and, once built, has obvious value to others in the market.

**What it requires:** Upfront capital and patience. The infrastructure revenue comes after the core product is established.

---

## Pattern 4: Local Manufacturing Arbitrage

- Import costs are high (tariffs, shipping, currency risk)
- Local manufacturing eliminates these, even if initial factory investment is large
- Tolaram: switched from importing to local manufacturing in 1995 specifically because import costs were killing margins

**When it works:** When your product has simple enough manufacturing requirements that local production is feasible, and when import costs meaningfully eat into margins.

**What it requires:** Factory investment, local workforce training, and potentially lobbying for import restrictions on competing products.

---

## Cost Structure Design Questions

1. What does the customer currently spend on workarounds? (Your price ceiling)
2. What is your raw material cost? Can it approach zero? (Waste-as-input)
3. What is your minimum viable volume for profitability?
4. Which parts of your cost structure are fixed vs. variable?
5. What infrastructure investment is needed, and can it generate independent revenue?
6. Are there import costs you can eliminate through local production?
7. What does the unit economics look like at 10x, 100x, 1000x current volume?

---

## Warning: The Subsidy Trap

Some social enterprises subsidize their product with grants or donations to hit affordable price points. This is NOT a disruptive cost structure — it's aid with extra steps.

A true disruptive cost structure is *self-sustaining*. The business model generates profit at the price point the customer can afford. If you need external funding to keep prices low, you haven't solved the cost structure problem — you've deferred it.

Grants and prizes (like the Earthshot Prize) are useful for R&D and initial scale, but the steady-state model must work on its own economics.
