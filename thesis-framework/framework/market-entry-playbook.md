# Market Entry Playbook

How to enter a market as a disruptive innovator — specifically in developing/emerging contexts like Nigeria.

---

## Step 1: Identify Non-consumption

Before picking a product, pick a *gap*. Look for:

- **People making do with terrible workarounds** — burning raw palm waste instead of processed fuel, using kerosene lamps, hand-washing when they'd prefer machines
- **People spending disproportionate income on basics** — 1/3 of income on cooking fuel, hours of labor on tasks that could be simplified
- **Products people actively seek despite poverty** — phones, motorcycles, certain foods. These reveal what people *pull* into their lives vs. what gets pushed on them

**The pull test:** Would people in this segment spend money on this if it existed at the right price? If yes, there's a market. If you'd have to convince them they need it, reconsider.

---

## Step 2: Choose Your Foothold

### Low-End Foothold
- Something similar already exists, but it's too expensive or inaccessible for your target
- Your version is stripped down, cheaper, "good enough"
- You enter at the bottom of an existing market and work up

**When to use:** When incumbents are over-serving mainstream customers and ignoring the bottom. When the product category is familiar but the price is wrong.

### New-Market Foothold
- Nothing like this exists for your target segment
- You're creating first-time buyers
- The category itself is new to them

**When to use:** When non-consumption is total — people don't have a worse version, they have *nothing*.

### Both (Strongest Position)
- Cheaper than existing alternatives AND a new category for your segment
- This is Indomie: cheaper than fast food (low-end) AND a food nobody had seen before (new-market)

---

## Step 3: Start with Distribution, Not Features

The biggest mistake is perfecting the product before solving distribution.

**Ask first:**
- How does this physically reach the customer?
- What infrastructure is missing between production and consumption?
- Who are the intermediaries, and do they exist yet?

**Tolaram's sequence:**
1. Imported 2 containers to test market fit
2. Proved demand existed
3. Built local factory (1995) to solve import cost problem
4. Built power/water infrastructure because it didn't exist
5. Built logistics fleet (1,000+ vehicles)
6. Trained local workforce in-house
7. Established 1,000 distributors → 600,000 retailers

The noodle recipe barely changed. The *system* around it was what scaled.

---

## Step 4: Plan for Infrastructure Gaps

In Nigeria specifically, do NOT assume:
- Reliable power
- Existing cold chain or logistics networks
- Trained workforce in your domain
- Government services that work on schedule

**Instead, plan to build or own:**
- Your own power/energy solution at production sites
- Your own logistics arm (which can later serve external clients — Tolaram's logistics now earns 65% from non-Tolaram customers)
- Your own training pipeline
- Your own quality control standards

See: [../nigeria-context/infrastructure-realities.md](../nigeria-context/infrastructure-realities.md)

---

## Step 5: Engage Government as a Partner, Not an Obstacle

Governments respond to demonstrated value. Tolaram's sequence:
1. Invested heavily, created jobs, built infrastructure
2. Used this leverage to lobby for import ban on competing noodles
3. Import ban locked in local manufacturing + quality standards
4. Tax contributions gave them further political standing
5. Lagos Free Zone project cemented their role as infrastructure partner

**The principle:** Build so much local value that the government has incentive to protect and support your operations.

See: [../nigeria-context/government-leverage.md](../nigeria-context/government-leverage.md)

---

## Step 6: Design for the Iteration Path

Your day-one product is deliberately minimal. But plan the upgrade path:

- **Phase 1:** Core product, maximum distribution, lowest viable price
- **Phase 2:** Local variants tailored to regional preferences
- **Phase 3:** Quality improvements, expanded product line
- **Phase 4:** Move upmarket, serve previously skeptical segments
- **Phase 5:** Adjacent innovations (Tolaram → logistics company → free trade zone)

Each phase should be funded by the previous one's revenue.
