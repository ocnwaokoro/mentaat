# Disqualification Criteria

What disruptive innovation is NOT. Use this as a fast filter before investing time in a full 8-criteria analysis.

---

## Automatic Disqualifiers

If ANY of these are true, the innovation is **not disruptive** (it may still be valuable — just call it what it is):

### 1. It targets mainstream customers who are already well-served
Disruption starts with the overlooked. If your primary customer already has a good solution at a price they can afford, you're doing sustaining or competitive innovation.

### 2. It enters at the top of the market
Disruption enters at the bottom (low-end) or outside (new-market). Premium entry = not disruption.

### 3. It's just a better version of what exists
Improving an existing product for existing customers is sustaining innovation. Apple didn't disrupt phones — they sustained/breakthrough-innovated on existing smartphones. The original cell phone disrupted landlines.

### 4. It requires convincing people they have a problem
If the target segment doesn't already feel the pain or already spend money on workarounds, you're pushing, not enabling pull. Disruption serves existing unmet demand — it doesn't create demand from scratch.

### 5. It only works with subsidies or grants
A disruptive cost structure is self-sustaining. If your price point only works with external funding, you haven't solved the cost problem.

### 6. It uses "disruptive" as a marketing label
Shaking up an industry or challenging incumbents doesn't automatically qualify. Uber disrupted taxis. Netflix disrupted Blockbuster. But many things labeled "disruptive" in pitch decks are simply competitive products in existing markets.

---

## Common Misclassifications

| Often Called Disruptive | Actually Is | Why |
|---|---|---|
| A better app for existing users | Sustaining innovation | Improves existing product for existing market |
| A premium product at lower price | Competitive innovation | Same market, same segment, price war |
| A tech solution to a problem nobody feels | Solution looking for a problem | Fails the pull test |
| An NGO-funded product distributed free | Aid/charity model | No self-sustaining cost structure |
| A product that "disrupts" incumbents' revenue | Competitive displacement | Disruption is about non-consumers, not stealing customers |

## The Quick Test

Answer these three questions:

1. **Who has nothing right now?** (If you can't name them, reconsider)
2. **Would they pay for this at a price you can sustain?** (If no, cost structure problem)
3. **Would incumbents ignore this segment?** (If incumbents would fight you immediately, you're competing, not disrupting)

If all three answers are clear and affirmative, proceed to the full 8-criteria checklist.

---

## Important Caveat

**Not being disruptive doesn't mean not being valuable.** Sustaining innovations, breakthrough innovations, and competitive innovations all create enormous value. The point of this filter isn't to kill ideas — it's to correctly categorize them so you apply the right strategy.

A sustaining innovation needs a different playbook than a disruptive one. Misapplying the disruptive playbook to a sustaining innovation (or vice versa) leads to strategic errors.
